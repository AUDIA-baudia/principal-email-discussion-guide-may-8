Context

Where this conversation fits

The email engagement is one side of an initial 90-day sprint that also includes a focus on enhancing the current acquisition strategy. The goal of the email project is to take Jennifer's current handling of your inboxes and turn it into a designed system that frees more of your attention for the work only you can do.

After your initial conversations with Brian about the importance of this project, we have spent considerable time with Jennifer documenting the current state. This conversation brings back what we have heard from you and from her, so we can confirm what we have right, surface where we are off, and capture the gaps. We are not trying to solve anything yet. We are capturing the current state, and the project plan we deliver after this is informed by what we hear today.

Part One

What we heard from Jennifer

Distilled from a discovery call (April 28) and a recap walkthrough (May 5). Captured in her own framing, not ours.

How she works today

Volume
Well over 100 per day on each side, with Jason's pile structurally larger because nothing gets filed.
Two patterns
She has built different rhythms for each of you. With Jason, she works verbally: he calls, she reads, he dictates a response, then they move on. With Kenneth, she uses Outlook color categories and watches the unread count, treating roughly twenty unread as the cue to stop other work and clear.
No formal training
Eighteen years on the job, no formal executive assistant or inbox management training. Every system she runs is one she invented.
Inbox-as-task-list
Jen's own task list lives in her inbox by deliberate choice. She has tried other tools and finds tasks get lost when she splits across platforms. Email plus Planner for the mall is her preferred surface area.
Open desk
She sits in an open seat that anyone can walk up to. Friday remote work is when the focused email work actually happens, because nobody can interrupt her.

What slows her down

  • Maintaining two divergent systems. Running one approach for Jason and another for Kenneth costs her time, but more importantly costs her accuracy. She hates making mistakes, and the switching between two principals is where mistakes happen.
  • Context switching across an open-desk workflow. Jennifer sits in an open seat and gets interrupted throughout the day, while running multiple parallel threads (executive support across both of you, the mall, marketing, accounting backup, design work). Each added concurrent responsibility roughly doubles the cognitive load, and the brain does not switch cleanly between divergent tasks. For a detail-oriented and task-oriented person, which she is per her Predictive Index profile, the visible result is occasional dropped balls on work she would otherwise handle cleanly. To be clear: Jennifer has not complained about this. She has only stated it as fact. We are naming it because it is the single largest hidden cost in the current setup.
  • Vendor and client response patterns. When she replies on behalf of either of you to mail you originally received, the response rate is fine. When she sends new outbound on your behalf, having one of you cc'd on the email materially speeds the reply.
  • PTO is not real PTO. She does not fully disconnect on time off. She has framed this partly as her own choice (she would rather skim daily than face 500-plus emails on return), but the underlying issue is she has no replaceable backup.

What she is asking for

  • Less time on email overall, not more. She wants her capacity back for the other work she does, including the creative and design work she enjoys.
  • Streamlining wherever the three of you can agree. She is not asking you to be the same as each other; she is asking for unification wherever you can both live with the same approach.
  • Documented authority. Clarity on what she handles end-to-end without checking in, what she escalates, and what she sends on behalf of one of you.
Part Two

How we are interpreting it

Three problems sitting under one label, plus an external pattern. Working framing only; this is where we want your reactions before it becomes the plan.

For Jason

Volume is downstream of attention. The challenge looks less like "too much email" and more like "email fragments my focus." Probable shape of the answer: a structured version of the verbal review you already do, on a predictable cadence.

For Kenneth

The categorization is largely working, with kinks Jennifer is still actively refining. The lever is a tighter triage layer plus expanded standing authority for Jennifer on repetitive responses, so the inbox you actually see is smaller and decision-ready.

For Jennifer

Documented authority, a unified system across both of you wherever you can agree, and a deliberate reduction in her email touch-time. The plan must give her time back, not consume more of it.

External convention

Less of a wholesale signaling redesign, more of a documented "cc the principal" convention for Jennifer's outbound on your behalf, since that is where the speed actually comes from.

Part Three

Questions for each of you, individually

We can run these as private 1:1 conversations, or in a joint setting where each of you answers in turn. Your call. The point is to hear from each of you on your own terms, before we ask you to align as a team.

For both of you
  1. Does what you read in Part One match what you would have said about how Jennifer works for you? Anything land off, missing, or out of balance?
  2. How do you see Jennifer's role today? The breadth of what she handles (executive support across both of you, marketing, Sharptown Mall coordination, accounting backup, graphic design, office tours, reception, food and refreshments for guests) is sometimes normal in a small-office administrator and EA hybrid, but rarely ideal. Has it grown by your design, or accumulated organically over her years here? This matters for how we scope the email engagement, because email is one piece of a much larger portfolio. The context-switching point we made in Part One is at its sharpest here.
  3. Where would you be willing to expand the standing authority you give Jennifer (things she could handle without checking in that she currently checks in on), and where will you not move at all (things you want to keep doing personally, no matter what)?
  4. How do you want to be involved in the design itself: light touch, hands-on, or fully hands-off until we bring you a draft?
  5. The problem is complex. The solution we bring back is likely to include adjustments to process, systems, technology, and possibly to roles or responsibilities. Are you comfortable receiving a project plan that may challenge your current assumptions on any of those?
Jason, specifically
  1. The verbal review pattern (you call, Jen reads, you dictate): is that working for you, or is it a workaround you would replace if we offered a better option?
  2. The thirty-windows-open pattern is the most distinctive thing we observed. When you think about your relationship to email, what is the real problem for you?
  3. Your inbox has accumulated over years. Does the size of it bother you, and what does "good" look like for your relationship to email ninety days from now? Be specific if you can.
  4. Email retention. Traci has historically cleared old mail from your inbox when you have asked her to. Our understanding is that you prefer email kept in place rather than deleted. We need a clean rule here so Jennifer knows what to do. What is your preference going forward?
Kenneth, specifically
  1. The twenty-unread anxiety threshold Jennifer described: is that a real number for you, or shorthand for something else?
  2. Your color categorization is largely working. Anything missing, any category that should change, or any category (like board email, since you sit on two) that is more stress-inducing than the others?
  3. What does "good" look like for your inbox ninety days from now? Put another way: what would tell you this engagement worked?
  4. The task list. Earlier in the relationship Jen was updating yours, and double-entry caused confusion. Where do you want to land on that going forward: Jen owns it, you own it, or shared with clear lines?
Part Four

Questions for the two of you, together

These are the questions where the answer matters more from the two of you in a room than from either of you separately.

For the two of you
  1. Would you be willing to make slight adjustments to your personal preferences so that we can land on one system that works for both of you, rather than asking Jennifer to run two?
  2. Is there anything I need to know right now that is a non-starter, anything I just cannot propose? For instance, changing Jennifer's responsibilities. There may be nothing. I want to ask while we are all here.
  3. Related to the previous question: are you okay with it if part of my recommendation involves adjustments to Jennifer's roles or responsibilities? I am not saying I will recommend that. I want to make sure that if I do, you are willing to consider it.
  4. I know you have answered a version of this for Brian, but I would like to hear it from each of you myself. If we fast-forward ninety days and the three-way collaboration with Jennifer is working great, what is different from today? In your words.
Part Five

What we do with your answers

We take what each of you tells us, integrate it with what Jennifer has already shared, and use it to build the draft 90-day project plan. We bring that plan back to you first. Once you feel good about the proposed solutions, the four of us walk through it with Jennifer together. The plan only moves with your sign-off.

The point of this conversation is to make sure the plan we build is one you actually want, designed around the way you actually work, not the way a consultant assumes executives should work.

Jason and Kenneth, thank you for the time on this. The strength of what we build over the next ninety days depends entirely on the honesty of the answers in this conversation.

Looking forward to it.
